<p><strong>Type A and Type B rootless voicings</strong> give jazz pianists two compact ways to arrange the same chord. The best choice is usually the shape that stays in a clear register and moves the shortest distance from the previous chord.</p>

<p>This guide reviews the two interval formulas, introduces a practical root-name shortcut from the original lesson, and applies the shapes to major ii-V-I progressions in all twelve keys.</p>

<h2 id="what-are-type-a-and-type-b-rootless-voicings">What Are Type A and Type B Rootless Voicings?</h2>

<p>In this system, a Type A voicing begins with the chord’s 3rd and a Type B voicing begins with its 7th. For a seventh chord with a natural 9th:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Type A:</strong> 3-5-7-9</li>
  <li><strong>Type B:</strong> 7-9-3-5</li>
</ul>

<p>For a dominant seventh chord with a 9th and 13th:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Type A:</strong> 3-13-b7-9</li>
  <li><strong>Type B:</strong> b7-9-3-13</li>
</ul>

<p>The root is omitted because a bass player or the harmonic context can supply it. The 3rd and 7th define chord quality and function, while the 9th and 13th add color.</p>

<p>You may also encounter spread rootless layouts written as <strong>3-7-9-5</strong> or <strong>7-3-5-9</strong>. They use the same essential note pool but distribute it differently. Compare the exact interval order instead of assuming every “Type A” or “Type B” label means the same shape.</p>

<aside><strong>Terminology note:</strong> A/B labels are not completely standardized. Some books reverse the names or describe the shapes by their exact interval order. Learn the formulas as well as the labels.</aside>

<h2 id="choose-the-shape-by-register-and-voice-leading">Choose the Shape by Register and Voice Leading</h2>

<p>Both inversions can describe the same chord, but they do not sit equally well in every register. If a Type A shape falls too low, its close intervals may sound muddy. If a Type B shape sits too high, it may collide with the melody or the right hand.</p>

<p>Use three questions:</p>

<ol>
  <li>Does the shape remain in a clear middle register?</li>
  <li>Does it keep common tones from the previous chord?</li>
  <li>Do the other voices move by step or small interval?</li>
</ol>

<p>This is the musical reason for alternating shapes through a progression. A new root does not require every note to jump to a new position.</p>

<h2 id="a-practical-root-name-shortcut">A Practical Root-Name Shortcut</h2>

<p>The original lesson gives a mnemonic for keeping these left-hand voicings in a useful range:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Roots named <strong>C or D</strong>, including their sharps or flats, usually use Type A.</li>
  <li>Roots named <strong>F, G, or A</strong>, including their sharps or flats, usually use Type B.</li>
  <li>Roots named <strong>B or E</strong> may use either type; choose the one that completes the smoothest progression.</li>
</ul>

<p>For example, Cmaj7(9), C7(9,13), Dm7(9), and D-flat7(9,13) use Type A in this register-based system. Fm7(9), F-sharp7(9,13), G7(9,13), and A-flatmaj7(9) use Type B.</p>

<aside><strong>Important:</strong> This root-name chart is a practical fingering shortcut for a chosen keyboard register, not a universal law of harmony. If the arrangement, melody, or ensemble requires another inversion, use the shape that sounds and connects best.</aside>

<h2 id="alternate-a-and-b-through-a-ii-v-i">Alternate A and B through a ii-V-I</h2>

<p>A major ii-V-I often alternates the two shapes:</p>

<p><strong>Type A - Type B - Type A</strong></p>

<p>or</p>

<p><strong>Type B - Type A - Type B</strong></p>

<p>In C major, Dm7-G7-Cmaj7 uses A-B-A in this system. In E-flat major, Fm7-Bb7-Ebmaj7 uses B-A-B.</p>

<p>Once the first chord is placed in a comfortable register, the alternating pattern gives you a reliable starting point for the following two chords. Always listen and check the actual notes rather than relying on the letters alone.</p>

<h2 id="applying-the-system-to-diatonic-chords">Applying the System to Diatonic Chords</h2>

<p>In C major, begin with the obvious root-name choices:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Cmaj7: A</li>
  <li>Dm7: A</li>
  <li>Fmaj7: B</li>
  <li>G7: B</li>
  <li>Am7: B</li>
</ul>

<p>The remaining Em7 and Bm7b5 depend on the progression and register. The original exercise assigns Em7 to A and Bm7b5 to B.</p>

<p>In F major, the root-name shortcut immediately gives Type B to Fmaj7, Gm7, and Am7, and Type A to C7 and Dm7. B-flatmaj7 and Em7b5 are then selected by context; the original exercise uses A for both.</p>

<p>These assignments are useful for drilling one connected layout, but real comping may change inversion to follow the melody, avoid another instrument, or create a different line.</p>

<h2 id="use-the-shapes-in-a-jazz-standard">Use the Shapes in a Jazz Standard</h2>

<p>After marking the diatonic chords in a tune, label each one A or B and play only the left-hand voicing. Then add the melody or improvise with the right hand.</p>

<p>If a chord is a secondary dominant or belongs to a temporary key, identify its function before choosing tensions. The same A/B interval layouts still work, but the available notes may differ.</p>

<h2 id="a-minimal-movement-practice-routine">A Minimal-Movement Practice Routine</h2>

<ol>
  <li>Play the ii chord in the recommended register.</li>
  <li>Hold any notes shared with V7.</li>
  <li>Move the remaining voices to the closest chord tones or tensions.</li>
  <li>Repeat the process from V7 to Imaj7.</li>
  <li>Transpose the progression around the circle of fourths.</li>
  <li>Record yourself and listen for jumps, muddy low clusters, or melody collisions.</li>
</ol>

<h2 id="summary">Summary</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Type A begins with the 3rd; Type B begins with the 7th.</li>
  <li>Choose the inversion that stays in a clear register and minimizes voice movement.</li>
  <li>A practical shortcut uses Type A for C/D roots and Type B for F/G/A roots.</li>
  <li>B/E roots are chosen by context in this register-based system.</li>
  <li>Major ii-V-I progressions often alternate A-B-A or B-A-B.</li>
  <li>The interval formulas matter more than the nonstandardized A/B labels.</li>
</ul>
