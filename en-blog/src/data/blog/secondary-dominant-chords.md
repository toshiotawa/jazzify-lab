<p><strong>Secondary dominant chords</strong> create a brief pull toward a chord other than the home tonic. They sound like the V7 chord of a temporary target, making an ordinary diatonic chord feel momentarily like a local tonic.</p>

<p>This guide explains how secondary dominants work in a major key, how to label them, how to find their expected resolutions, and how to recognize them in jazz standards.</p>

<h2 id="what-is-a-secondary-dominant">What Is a Secondary Dominant?</h2>

<p>The dominant seventh chord built on the fifth degree of the home key is the <strong>primary dominant</strong>. In C major, that chord is G7, and it normally resolves to C.</p>

<p>A secondary dominant is a dominant seventh chord that targets another diatonic chord. It is “secondary” because it belongs to a temporary key area rather than serving as the main dominant of the song.</p>

<p>For example, Dm7 is the ii chord in C major. The dominant of D is A7, so A7 can be used before Dm7:</p>

<p><strong>A7-Dm7</strong></p>

<p>A7 contains C-sharp, a note outside the C major scale. That chromatic note creates a leading-tone pull into D. The progression briefly makes Dm sound like a tonic without necessarily changing the overall key.</p>

<aside><strong>Quick note:</strong> A secondary dominant is best understood by its target. When you see an unexpected dominant seventh chord, look a perfect fourth above its root to find the chord it is trying to tonicize.</aside>

<h2 id="the-five-common-secondary-dominants-in-a-major-key">The Five Common Secondary Dominants in a Major Key</h2>

<p>In C major, the most common temporary targets are ii, iii, IV, V, and vi. Build the V7 of each target to find the five secondary dominants:</p>

<table>
  <thead>
    <tr><th>Label</th><th>Chord in C major</th><th>Expected target</th></tr>
  </thead>
  <tbody>
    <tr><td>V7/ii</td><td>A7</td><td>Dm or Dm7</td></tr>
    <tr><td>V7/iii</td><td>B7</td><td>Em or Em7</td></tr>
    <tr><td>V7/IV</td><td>C7</td><td>F or Fmaj7</td></tr>
    <tr><td>V7/V</td><td>D7</td><td>G or G7</td></tr>
    <tr><td>V7/vi</td><td>E7</td><td>Am or Am7</td></tr>
  </tbody>
</table>

<p>The vii chord is usually not tonicized this way because its diminished quality does not provide a stable temporary tonic. The home tonic is already served by the primary dominant, G7, so that chord is simply V7 rather than a secondary dominant.</p>

<h3>Why minor targets still use a dominant seventh chord</h3>

<p>When the temporary target is minor, use the dominant from the target’s harmonic minor scale. For example, the natural D minor scale would produce Am7, but harmonic minor raises C to C-sharp and produces A7. That raised leading tone is what creates the strong resolution to Dm.</p>

<h2 id="how-secondary-dominants-are-labeled">How Secondary Dominants Are Labeled</h2>

<p>A7 in C major is written <strong>V7/ii</strong>, read as “five-seven of two.” The symbol tells you that A7 is the dominant seventh chord of the ii chord.</p>

<p>Some materials write <strong>V7 of ii</strong> instead of using a slash. The meaning is the same:</p>

<ul>
  <li>The symbol after the slash names the target.</li>
  <li>The V7 before the slash describes the chord’s function relative to that target.</li>
</ul>

<p>To decode D7 as V7/V in C major, work from right to left. The target is V, or G. The dominant seventh chord of G is D7.</p>

<h2 id="how-to-find-a-secondary-dominant">How to Find a Secondary Dominant</h2>

<p>Use this process when you are given a target chord:</p>

<ol>
  <li>Identify the root of the target.</li>
  <li>Move up a perfect fifth, or down a perfect fourth, to find its dominant root.</li>
  <li>Build a dominant seventh chord on that root.</li>
  <li>Label the result as V7 followed by a slash and the target’s Roman numeral.</li>
</ol>

<p>To find V7/vi in C, first identify vi as A minor. A perfect fifth above A is E, so E7 is V7/vi.</p>

<p>You can also work backward from an unfamiliar dominant chord. If you find B7 in a passage in C major, move a perfect fourth upward from B to E. Em is iii in C, so B7 is V7/iii.</p>

<h2 id="expected-resolution-and-deceptive-motion">Expected Resolution and Deceptive Motion</h2>

<p>A secondary dominant strongly suggests its target, but it does not have to resolve there. A7 usually moves to Dm in C major, yet a composer can redirect it. When the expected tonic resolution is avoided, the result may be described as a deceptive resolution.</p>

<p>This is why analysis should account for both chord construction and musical context. A dominant seventh quality suggests a possible target; the following chord tells you whether the expected tonicization is fulfilled.</p>

<h2 id="secondary-dominant-practice-questions">Secondary Dominant Practice Questions</h2>

<p>What is V7/ii in F major?</p>
<details>
  <summary>Show the answer</summary>
  <p>The ii chord is Gm. Its dominant is D7, so the answer is D7.</p>
</details>

<p>What is V7/vi in G major?</p>
<details>
  <summary>Show the answer</summary>
  <p>The vi chord is Em. Its dominant is B7, so the answer is B7.</p>
</details>

<p>In F major, is D7 a secondary dominant?</p>
<details>
  <summary>Show the answer</summary>
  <p>Yes. D7 resolves toward Gm, the ii chord, so it is V7/ii.</p>
</details>

<p>In B-flat major, is E7 a standard secondary dominant?</p>
<details>
  <summary>Show the answer</summary>
  <p>No. E7 would target A, but A is not one of the usual diatonic tonicization targets in B-flat major.</p>
</details>

<h2 id="finding-secondary-dominants-in-a-jazz-standard">Finding Secondary Dominants in a Jazz Standard</h2>

<p>When analyzing a lead sheet, first mark every dominant seventh chord. Then ask whether each one is the primary V7 or a dominant of another diatonic chord.</p>

<p>In a C-major setting, the original article uses a passage from <cite>Fly Me to the Moon</cite> to illustrate three examples:</p>

<ul>
  <li>C7 is V7/IV and points toward F.</li>
  <li>E7 is V7/vi and points toward Am.</li>
  <li>A7 is V7/ii and points toward Dm.</li>
</ul>

<h2 id="summary">Summary</h2>

<ul>
  <li>A secondary dominant is a V7 chord that tonicizes a chord other than the home tonic.</li>
  <li>In a major key, the usual targets are ii, iii, IV, V, and vi.</li>
  <li>V7/V means “the dominant seventh chord of the dominant.”</li>
  <li>Move a perfect fourth above a dominant root to find its expected target.</li>
  <li>Minor targets use the dominant produced by harmonic minor.</li>
  <li>The expected target can be delayed or avoided in a deceptive resolution.</li>
</ul>
