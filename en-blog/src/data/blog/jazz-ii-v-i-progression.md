<p>The <strong>ii V I progression</strong> is one of the most common harmonic patterns in jazz standards. Once you can build it in every key and recognize it on a lead sheet, many seemingly complicated chord progressions become easier to understand.</p>

<p>This guide explains the function of the three chords, how to find them in a major key, how the dominant resolves, and how to spot ii-V-I movement that briefly points to a key other than the song’s home key.</p>

<h2 id="what-is-a-ii-v-i-progression">What Is a ii-V-I Progression?</h2>

<p>In a major key, the ii-V-I progression uses the seventh chords built on the second, fifth, and first degrees of the major scale:</p>

<p><strong>iim7-V7-Imaj7</strong></p>

<p>In C major, those chords are:</p>

<p><strong>Dm7-G7-Cmaj7</strong></p>

<p>You may see the progression written as <strong>ii-V-I</strong>, <strong>II-V-I</strong>, <strong>2-5-1</strong>, or simply <strong>ii-V</strong> when the tonic chord is omitted or delayed. Lowercase Roman numerals are often used for minor-quality chords, so <strong>ii</strong> immediately tells you that the second chord is minor.</p>

<h2 id="why-the-ii-v-i-sounds-resolved">Why the ii-V-I Sounds Resolved</h2>

<p>Each chord has a clear harmonic role:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>ii</strong> has a predominant function. It prepares the dominant.</li>
  <li><strong>V7</strong> has a dominant function. It creates tension that points toward the tonic.</li>
  <li><strong>Imaj7</strong> has a tonic function. It provides the point of arrival.</li>
</ul>

<p>The root movement contributes to the strong sense of direction. D moves up a perfect fourth to G, and G moves up a perfect fourth to C. You can also hear this as descending fifths.</p>

<p>The dominant chord contains another powerful resolution. In G7, B is the third and F is the seventh. B tends to rise to C, while F tends to fall to E. These two half-step resolutions lead directly into the third and root of Cmaj7.</p>

<p>You do not need advanced voicings to hear the function. Start with simple seventh chords and listen for preparation, tension, and release.</p>

<h2 id="how-to-build-a-ii-v-i-in-any-major-key">How to Build a ii-V-I in Any Major Key</h2>

<p>Use this three-step process:</p>

<ol>
  <li>Write the major scale of the target key.</li>
  <li>Find scale degrees 2, 5, and 1.</li>
  <li>Build a minor seventh chord on 2, a dominant seventh chord on 5, and a major seventh chord on 1.</li>
</ol>

<p>For example, the F major scale is F-G-A-B-flat-C-D-E. Its second, fifth, and first degrees are G, C, and F, so the progression is:</p>

<p><strong>Gm7-C7-Fmaj7</strong></p>

<p>The G major scale is G-A-B-C-D-E-F-sharp. Its ii-V-I is:</p>

<p><strong>Am7-D7-Gmaj7</strong></p>

<h2 id="how-to-recognize-ii-v-i-progressions-in-jazz-standards">How to Recognize ii-V-I Progressions in Jazz Standards</h2>

<p>A jazz standard may be in C major while briefly using a ii-V-I progression from another key. For example, you might find Gm7-C7-Fmaj7 inside a tune whose home key is C. Those three chords point temporarily to F major.</p>

<p>This does not always mean the entire song has permanently changed key. It may be a short tonicization: the progression makes F sound like a local tonic before the harmony returns to C.</p>

<p>To find these patterns quickly, look for either of these clues:</p>

<ul>
  <li>A dominant seventh chord preceded by a minor seventh chord whose root is a perfect fourth below it.</li>
  <li>A minor seventh chord followed by a dominant seventh chord whose root is a perfect fourth above it.</li>
</ul>

<p>Then ask where the dominant chord wants to resolve. A C7 usually points to some kind of F chord. If the complete pattern is Gm7-C7-Fmaj7, you can label it ii-V-I in F.</p>

<h3>Recognition exercises</h3>

<p>If Gm7 is the ii chord, what are the V and I chords?</p>
<details>
  <summary>Show the answer</summary>
  <p>Gm7 is ii in F major, so the complete progression is Gm7-C7-Fmaj7.</p>
</details>

<p>If D7 is the V chord, what is the ii chord?</p>
<details>
  <summary>Show the answer</summary>
  <p>D7 is V in G, so the ii chord is Am7.</p>
</details>

<p>Is Bm7-Eb7 a diatonic ii-V pair?</p>
<details>
  <summary>Show the answer</summary>
  <p>No. The root of a diatonic V chord should be a perfect fourth above the root of ii; B up to E-flat is not a perfect fourth.</p>
</details>

<h2 id="a-practical-ii-v-i-piano-routine">A Practical ii-V-I Piano Routine</h2>

<ol>
  <li>Choose one major key and say the scale degrees aloud: “two, five, one.”</li>
  <li>Name the three chord roots before playing them.</li>
  <li>Play the seventh chords slowly and listen for the dominant resolving to the tonic.</li>
  <li>Repeat the pattern around the circle of fourths until you have covered all twelve keys.</li>
  <li>Open a lead sheet and circle every minor-seventh-to-dominant-seventh pair.</li>
  <li>Label the temporary tonic that each dominant chord targets.</li>
</ol>

<p>At first, prioritize recognition over speed. The aim is to see Dm7-G7 and immediately understand its function, not merely to remember the chord names. Once that recognition is automatic, you can apply different voicings and improvisation ideas to the same harmonic unit.</p>
