<p>The altered scale is one of the most useful sounds for creating tension over a dominant seventh chord. This guide explains how to build it, how its interval names change in a dominant context, and how to connect it to melodic minor, Mixolydian b6, and the tritone substitute.</p>

<h2 id="what-is-the-altered-scale">What Is the Altered Scale?</h2>

<p>Start with a major scale and lower every note except the tonic by a half step. The resulting formula is <strong>1, b2, b3, b4, b5, b6, b7</strong>. This spelling is often called the <strong>Super Locrian scale</strong>.</p>

<figure>
  <img src="https://jazzify-cdn.com/blog/altered-scale/images/43a77c3707c109dbd951c1629fb44e69.png" alt="C altered scale written as 1 b2 b3 b4 b5 b6 b7" width="806" height="197" loading="lazy" decoding="async">
  <figcaption>C altered: C, D-flat, E-flat, F-flat, G-flat, A-flat, B-flat.</figcaption>
</figure>

<aside><strong>Quick note:</strong> The altered scale looks simple as a scale formula, but its real value comes from hearing each note as a color against a dominant seventh chord.</aside>

<h2 id="which-chord-uses-the-altered-scale">Which Chord Uses the Altered Scale?</h2>

<p>Stacking the scale in thirds can suggest Cm7b5, but the altered scale is normally played over a <strong>dominant seventh chord with altered tensions</strong>. Over C7, the scale contains the chord tones C, E, and B-flat together with D-flat, E-flat, G-flat, and A-flat.</p>

<figure>
  <img src="https://jazzify-cdn.com/blog/altered-scale/images/85a8cfa42282fe044db65b0dd1ae7ed3.png" alt="C altered scale compared with Cm7b5 and C7" width="796" height="302" loading="lazy" decoding="async">
  <figcaption>The pitch collection can form Cm7b5 in thirds, yet its standard functional use is over C7.</figcaption>
</figure>

<p>The important enharmonic change is <strong>F-flat = E</strong>. In a C7 context, that note is the major third. This is why the scale can support a dominant chord even though the Super Locrian spelling uses b4.</p>

<h2 id="altered-scale-tension-notation">Altered Scale Tension Notation</h2>

<p>Jazz musicians usually respell the altered scale to show its relationship to the dominant chord:</p>

<p><strong>1, b9, #9, 3, b5 (or #11), b13, b7</strong></p>

<figure>
  <img src="https://jazzify-cdn.com/blog/altered-scale/images/010e590ef421efef0b58c1fc36c92eca.png" alt="C altered scale respelled with dominant chord tensions" width="920" height="279" loading="lazy" decoding="async">
  <figcaption>The same notes are respelled as chord tones and altered tensions over C7.</figcaption>
</figure>

<p>The tension spelling contains both b9 and #9 while omitting the natural fifth. That asymmetry is part of the sound: the scale concentrates unstable colors around the third and seventh, making the resolution to the tonic especially strong.</p>

<h2 id="the-seventh-mode-of-melodic-minor">The Seventh Mode of Melodic Minor</h2>

<p>The altered scale is the seventh mode of melodic minor. To find the parent melodic minor scale, move up a half step from the altered-scale root. For example:</p>

<ul>
  <li>G altered uses the notes of A-flat melodic minor.</li>
  <li>B altered uses the notes of C melodic minor.</li>
  <li>D altered uses the notes of E-flat melodic minor.</li>
  <li>G-sharp altered uses the notes of A melodic minor.</li>
</ul>

<blockquote><strong>Practice tip:</strong> Pick a dominant chord, move its root up a half step, and play melodic minor from that new note. Then return to the dominant root and hear the same pitch collection as an altered scale.</blockquote>

<h2 id="using-the-altered-scale-over-v7">Using the Altered Scale over V7</h2>

<p>In a Dm7-G7-Cmaj7 progression, play G altered over G7. The parent collection is A-flat melodic minor, but you do not need to hear the chord as a borrowed VII7 in A-flat. Functionally it is still the V7 of C; the parent scale is simply a practical way to organize the available tensions.</p>

<h3>Why C modal interchange does not explain G altered</h3>

<p>G altered does not contain C, so it cannot be described as a mode with C as its tonic. A more useful comparison is to reorder the same notes from E-flat or to connect them directly to the dominant function.</p>

<h2 id="g-altered-and-e-flat-mixolydian-b6">G Altered and E-flat Mixolydian b6</h2>

<p>Reorder the notes of G altered from E-flat and you get <strong>E-flat Mixolydian b6</strong>. G is the third of that mode, so G7 can be heard as III7 within the same pitch collection.</p>

<figure>
  <img src="https://jazzify-cdn.com/blog/altered-scale/images/8e6d458ede9d106474dd65f2ce9f034f.png" alt="G altered and E-flat Mixolydian b6 share the same notes" width="1007" height="402" loading="lazy" decoding="async">
  <figcaption>G altered and E-flat Mixolydian b6 are rotations of the same seven notes.</figcaption>
</figure>

<h2 id="from-the-altered-scale-to-the-tritone-substitute">From the Altered Scale to the Tritone Substitute</h2>

<p>G7 and D-flat7 are tritone substitutes. The notes of G altered are the same pitch collection as D-flat Lydian dominant. That means a line learned over G7 altered can also work over D-flat7 when the harmony uses the tritone substitute.</p>

<figure>
  <img src="https://jazzify-cdn.com/blog/altered-scale/images/cae0e079bf8b10a8448914dc944db026.png" alt="G altered compared with D-flat Lydian dominant over tritone substitute chords" width="1076" height="636" loading="lazy" decoding="async">
  <figcaption>E-flat Mixolydian b6, G altered, and D-flat Lydian dominant organize the same pitch collection from different roots.</figcaption>
</figure>

<h2 id="summary">Summary</h2>

<ul>
  <li>The Super Locrian formula is 1, b2, b3, b4, b5, b6, b7.</li>
  <li>Over V7, respell it as 1, b9, #9, 3, b5/#11, b13, b7.</li>
  <li>The altered scale is the seventh mode of melodic minor.</li>
  <li>G altered uses the notes of A-flat melodic minor.</li>
  <li>G altered and D-flat Lydian dominant use the same notes, which connects the scale to the tritone substitute.</li>
</ul>
