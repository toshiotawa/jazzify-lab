<p>The seven <strong>jazz modes for piano</strong> come from treating each note of a major scale as a new tonal center. The notes may stay the same, but the interval pattern, home note, chord connection, and overall sound change.</p>

<p>This practical guide covers Ionian, Dorian, Phrygian, Lydian, Mixolydian, Aeolian, and Locrian. You will learn each interval formula, find its parent major scale, connect it to a seventh chord, and practice hearing it as a distinct sound.</p>

<h2 id="what-are-the-seven-modes-of-the-major-scale">What Are the Seven Modes of the Major Scale?</h2>

<p>Play the notes of C major from each successive scale degree and you get seven modes:</p>

<ul>
  <li>C to C: C Ionian</li>
  <li>D to D: D Dorian</li>
  <li>E to E: E Phrygian</li>
  <li>F to F: F Lydian</li>
  <li>G to G: G Mixolydian</li>
  <li>A to A: A Aeolian</li>
  <li>B to B: B Locrian</li>
</ul>

<figure>
  <img src="https://jazzify-cdn.com/blog/jazz-modes-for-piano/images/9329ec16e69646a0bdd7a73f5160e048.png" alt="Staff notation showing C Ionian, D Dorian, E Phrygian, F Lydian, G Mixolydian, A Aeolian, and B Locrian using the notes of C major" width="866" height="263" loading="lazy" decoding="async">
  <figcaption>The seven white-note modes run from C Ionian through B Locrian, each beginning on its own modal root.</figcaption>
</figure>

<p>Simply starting and ending on a different note is not enough to create a convincing modal sound. You need to hear that note as home, support it with the appropriate chord, and emphasize the interval that distinguishes the mode.</p>

<p>The formulas are measured from the modal root. For example, D Dorian uses D-E-F-G-A-B-C. Its b3 and b7 make it minor, while its natural 6 distinguishes it from D Aeolian.</p>

<h2 id="ionian-the-major-scale-sound">Ionian: The Major-Scale Sound</h2>

<p>Ionian is the ordinary major scale. C Ionian is C-D-E-F-G-A-B. Its natural 3 and major 7 establish the bright major quality.</p>

<p>Over a sustained Cmaj7 chord, emphasize B resolving to C. F, the natural 4, can create a minor-ninth rub above E when sustained in a voicing, so pianists often treat it as a melodic passing tone in a plain Ionian context.</p>

<h2 id="dorian-minor-with-a-natural-6">Dorian: Minor with a Natural 6</h2>

<p>D Dorian uses the notes of C major but centers them on D: D-E-F-G-A-B-C. The natural 6, B, is the defining color. Compare it with D natural minor, which contains B-flat.</p>

<p>Dorian is one of the most important modes in jazz. It is commonly associated with minor seventh chords, including the ii chord in a major-key ii-V-I progression and many modal minor passages.</p>

<h2 id="phrygian-minor-with-a-flat-2">Phrygian: Minor with a Flat 2</h2>

<p>E Phrygian is E-F-G-A-B-C-D. Its b2, F, creates an immediately recognizable dark tension against the root E.</p>

<p>Because the b2 is so strong, sustain an E bass note while alternating between E and F in the right hand. This makes the mode sound different from Aeolian or Dorian even though all three have a minor 3rd and minor 7th.</p>

<h2 id="lydian-major-with-a-sharp-4">Lydian: Major with a Sharp 4</h2>

<p>F Lydian is F-G-A-B-C-D-E. The note B is #4 relative to F. That raised fourth avoids the natural-4 clash found between B-flat and A in F Ionian, creating the spacious maj7#11 color used frequently in jazz.</p>

<p>Practice an Fmaj7 chord in the left hand and play B prominently in the right. The #11 should sound like a defining color, not an accidental note outside the chord.</p>

<h2 id="mixolydian-major-with-a-flat-7">Mixolydian: Major with a Flat 7</h2>

<p>G Mixolydian is G-A-B-C-D-E-F. Its major 3 and b7 outline a dominant seventh chord. That makes Mixolydian the basic mode associated with an unaltered dominant chord.</p>

<p>Play G7 in the left hand and improvise with the white keys while treating G as home. Listen to how F, the b7, gives the mode a different pull from G Ionian, which would contain F-sharp.</p>

<h2 id="aeolian-the-natural-minor-scale">Aeolian: The Natural Minor Scale</h2>

<p>A Aeolian is A-B-C-D-E-F-G, the natural minor scale. Its b6, F, distinguishes it from A Dorian, which contains F-sharp.</p>

<p>Aeolian works for a natural-minor sound, but functional minor harmony often changes scale degrees to create a dominant chord. That is why harmonic and melodic minor become important beyond this first modal overview.</p>

<h2 id="locrian-minor-with-a-flat-5">Locrian: Minor with a Flat 5</h2>

<p>B Locrian is B-C-D-E-F-G-A. Its b3, b5, and b7 outline Bm7b5, while the b2 adds further instability.</p>

<p>In tonal jazz, Locrian is most often connected to a half-diminished chord rather than used as a long static tonic sound. Practice it over Bm7b5 and listen for the distinctive tritone from B to F.</p>

<h2 id="how-to-find-the-parent-major-scale">How to Find the Parent Major Scale</h2>

<p>Every major-scale mode uses the notes of a parent major scale. To build a mode on any root, ask which degree of the parent scale that root represents.</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Ionian:</strong> the modal root is degree 1, so the parent major scale has the same root.</li>
  <li><strong>Dorian:</strong> the modal root is degree 2, so the parent major root is a whole step below.</li>
  <li><strong>Phrygian:</strong> the modal root is degree 3, so the parent major root is a major third below.</li>
  <li><strong>Lydian:</strong> the modal root is degree 4, so the parent major root is a perfect fourth below.</li>
  <li><strong>Mixolydian:</strong> the modal root is degree 5, so the parent major root is a perfect fifth below.</li>
  <li><strong>Aeolian:</strong> the modal root is degree 6, so the parent major root is a major sixth below, or a minor third above.</li>
  <li><strong>Locrian:</strong> the modal root is degree 7, so the parent major root is a major seventh below, or a minor second above.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Example: E Dorian</h3>

<p>Dorian is the second mode. If E is degree 2, the parent major scale begins one whole step below on D. Play D major from E to E:</p>

<p><strong>E-F#-G-A-B-C#-D</strong></p>

<h3>Example: C Phrygian</h3>

<p>Phrygian is the third mode. If C is degree 3, the parent major scale begins a major third below on A-flat. Play A-flat major from C to C:</p>

<p><strong>C-Db-Eb-F-G-Ab-Bb</strong></p>

<h2 id="a-practical-modal-piano-routine">A Practical Modal Piano Routine</h2>

<ol>
  <li>Choose one modal root and hold it as a low drone.</li>
  <li>Build the mode using its interval formula.</li>
  <li>Play the associated seventh chord in the left hand.</li>
  <li>Improvise a short right-hand phrase that emphasizes the characteristic note.</li>
  <li>Compare the mode with another mode of the same root.</li>
  <li>Transpose the exercise to a new root.</li>
</ol>

<p>For example, compare C Ionian, C Lydian, and C Mixolydian over suitable C chords. The root stays fixed while scale degrees 4 and 7 change. Hearing those differences is more useful than only reciting the parent-scale names.</p>

<h2 id="summary">Summary</h2>

<ul>
  <li>The seven major-scale modes are Ionian, Dorian, Phrygian, Lydian, Mixolydian, Aeolian, and Locrian.</li>
  <li>Each mode has a distinct interval formula measured from its own root.</li>
  <li>A mode becomes audible when its root functions as the tonal center.</li>
  <li>Dorian highlights natural 6, Phrygian b2, Lydian #4, Mixolydian b7, Aeolian b6, and Locrian b2/b5.</li>
  <li>Use the parent major scale to find the notes, then practice the mode over its associated chord.</li>
  <li>A drone and characteristic-note exercise helps turn theory into a recognizable sound.</li>
</ul>
